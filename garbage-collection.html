Garbage collection and Recycling

Garbage collection

For city-scale garbage collection and recycling in Prajnapuri, garbage collection is a major task to keep the city clean. The approach focuses on two key goals:

  • How to keep the city clean.
  • How to recycle, reuse, decompose and process collected garbage so material is recovered and not wasted.

We apply DAA (Design & Analysis of Algorithms) techniques to optimize collection and recycling with respect to resources, cost, route, and time so performance remains acceptable across the city.

Steps

  1. Model the city as a graph: represent the road network and bins using a structured graph where:
    • Vertices: depots, garbage bins / waste containers, transfer stations, recycling plants, landfills.
    • Edges: roads, with weights such as travel time or fuel cost.
    • Attach attributes to nodes: location, capacity, current fill level, waste bin type (wet, dry, plastic, glass, metal), service time.
    • Model vehicles: capacity, type (recyclables vehicle vs general waste), assigned service time per area, cost per km/hour.
    • Data structures used: graphs (for DFS/BFS traversal), hash maps for bin metadata keyed by bin ID, and segment trees / Fenwick trees for quick updates to aggregated metrics.
  2. Compute distances and base routes: precompute shortest paths on the road network. Use Dijkstra from depots (example: garbage yard located at a known city corner) and from key nodes to build a distance matrix stored as a 2D array (u, v). For small areas use Floyd–Warshall for all-pairs shortest paths. This distance matrix is the input to higher-level routing algorithms.
  3. Vehicle types and constraints: plan vehicle fleets (trucks, mini-trucks) with limited capacities and waste-type restrictions. Optimize routes to minimize distance/cost while respecting constraints such as time windows (e.g. collect between 08:00–12:00 for certain zones) and per-vehicle capacity.

Recycling

After collection, separate and recycle materials where possible. We use algorithmic techniques to filter and categorize collected waste for further processing.

  • Use pattern/filtering algorithms to categorize items for recycling (example: use string/sequence filtering techniques such as Boyer–Moore for fast pattern detection when applicable to sensor or label data).
  • Sort and route recyclable materials to appropriate recycling plants or local hubs.
  • Track throughput and reuse rates with simple hash maps and aggregate structures for reporting.
